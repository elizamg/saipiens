### Evidence-based Instructional Shifts:

-   **Retrieval Practice**: Instead of re-reading notes, students should be tested frequently. The "Testing Effect" shows that the act of recalling information strengthens long-term memory far more than passive study.

-   **Spaced Repetition**: Research suggests that "spiraling" the curriculum---returning to key concepts over weeks or months---is significantly more effective than "massed practice" (cramming a unit and moving on).

-   **High-Dosage Tutoring**: Consistent, small-group tutoring (3+ times a week) is currently one of the most effective "catch-up" strategies supported by research to bridge the achievement gap.

-   **Interleaving**: Instead of "blocking" practice (doing 20 long-division problems in a row), teachers should mix different types of problems together. This forces the brain to first identify which strategy to use before applying it.

-   **Elaborative Interrogation**: This involves asking students "why" and "how" questions rather than "what" questions. By forcing a student to explain the underlying logic of a fact, they connect new information to their existing knowledge base (schema).

-   **Feedback Loops**: The most effective feedback isn't a grade at the end of a unit; it's the "mid-flight" correction

-   **The "No-Stakes" Quiz**: Using tools like exit tickets or polling apps allows teachers to see exactly where the class is confused in real-time.

-   **Worked Examples**: For complex tasks (like solving $x^2 + bx + c = 0$), research shows that showing a fully completed step-by-step example before asking students to solve one themselves reduces frustration and speeds up the transition to independent mastery.

-   **Fading Scaffolds**: Effective instruction starts with heavy support (graphic organizers, sentence starters) that is systematically "faded" out as the student gains competence.

### Benefits of 1:1 Tutoring:

-   **The "Error Correction" Loop**: A tutor catches a mistake at the exact moment it happens. They can say, "Wait, look at how you handled that negative sign in step two.

-   **Neural Efficiency**: Immediate feedback prevents the brain from reinforcing a "faulty circuit." This significantly reduces the time needed to achieve mastery.

-   **Gap Filling**: If a student is struggling with Chemistry because they actually forgot 8th-grade Algebra, a tutor can pause the lesson to fix that specific 8th-grade gap. A classroom teacher usually can't afford to stop the whole group for one person.

-   **Tailored Difficulty**: The tutor keeps the student in the Zone of Proximal Development. If the task is too easy, the tutor ramps it up instantly; if the student is hitting a wall, the tutor provides a "hint" (a scaffold) just enough to let the student finish the problem.

-   **Active Processing**: In a tutoring session, the student is "on the hook" the entire time. They cannot hide in the back of the room.

-   **Oral Defense**: Tutors often ask students to "teach it back" to them. Explaining a concept out loud forces the student to organize their thoughts in a way that just listening to a lecture never does.

-   **Focused Attention**: Tutoring removes extraneous cognitive load. The student doesn't have to worry about what their friends think, the noise in the hallway, or the teacher's pace.

-   **Working Memory Support**: A tutor acts as an external "working memory." They can hold the "big picture" of the problem while the student focuses on a specific, difficult calculation, preventing the student's brain from becoming overwhelmed.

Notes--Useful Ideas/Features:
----------------------

-   **Spaced Repetition/Spacing Effect/Distributed Practice**: Each homework should have a module covering past material (potentially based on the student's previous responses/competence)

-   **Interleaving**: Ideally we wouldn't label the problems with their topic so the student has to identify which strategy to use

-   **Elaborative Interrogation**: We should ask students "why" and "how" questions, since explaining the underlying logic of a fact or concept is shown to be helpful for learning

-   **Error Correction**: Correct an error as a student is writing their answer to save their time

-   **Gap Filling**: Identify and teach prerequisite concepts that the student might be missing

-   **Tailored Difficulty**: Allow the student to control their difficulty and encourage them to do problems at "the Zone of Proximal Development"

-   **Scaffolding**: Give scaffolding when the student gets stuck or requests help (e.g. next step/where to go next)

-   **Oral Defense**: "Teach it back" problems at the end of a homework (e.g. selected based on things the student was having difficulty with, along with some random ones to encourage retention of all the material since it might be "tested" on).

-   **Working Memory Support and Fading Scaffolding**: For "easy" difficulty problems, work through the problem with scaffolding along the way, so the student learns the scaffolding and can focus on each individual part of the problem
